I study the last moments before two black holes or neutron stars spiral into one another — and the gravitational waves that ripple outward from that collision, stretching and squeezing spacetime itself.

My work sits between simulation and detection: I model what a merger's waveform should look like, then search real interferometer data for that signature buried in the noise. A confirmed detection needs the same chirp showing up in two detectors, thousands of kilometers apart, within milliseconds of each other — that coincidence is what turns a noisy wiggle into a discovery.

Outside of research, I maintain a small open-source toolkit for gravitational-wave data conditioning, and I mentor early-career researchers moving from physics into astronomy.
